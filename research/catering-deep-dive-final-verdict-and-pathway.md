# Part 1 — Corporate catering: the same deep treatment

## 1. The three layers, catering version

| Layer | Catering version |
|---|---|
| **Product** | Same buyer-side negotiator engine |
| **Hackathon vertical** | Recurring corporate lunch for ~50 employees, quoted across 3 caterers |
| **Business** | B2B recurring procurement — office managers who order weekly |

## 2. The industry — it's the biggest of the three, by far

- **Market size:** US catering services ~$60.4B (2022) heading to **$109.4B by 2030**; the workplace/office segment is the fastest-growing slice at **8.8% CAGR** ([Grand View Research](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/us-catering-services-market-report), [press release](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/press-release/us-catering-services-market-analysis)). Compare: junk removal ~$10.7B, auto glass ~$9–20B.
- **Fragmentation:** genuinely extreme — **no US caterer holds more than 5% share** ([IBISWorld](https://www.ibisworld.com/united-states/industry/caterers/1682/)); thousands of regional operators.
- **Purchase frequency is the standout:** **51% of catered meetings and 81% of office catering orders recur weekly or more** ([ezCater](https://www.ezcater.com/lunchrush/restaurant/introduction-to-catering-how-big-is-the-opportunity/)). Junk removal and auto glass are one-off consumer purchases; catering is a weekly repeat buy. On pure business-model terms this is the best market of the three.
- **Real hidden fees exist:** the per-person price ($15–$40 for corporate lunch) routinely excludes the **service fee (5–20%)**, delivery fee, sales tax, gratuity (10–20%), and staffing/setup ([ezCater fee guide](https://www.ezcater.com/lunchrush/office/catering-order-minimums-service-fees/), [Spork Bytes](https://www.sporkbytes.com/corporate-event-catering-cost/), [Toast](https://pos.toasttab.com/blog/on-the-line/how-much-to-tip-for-catering)). A "$18/head" quote for 50 people is $900 on paper and ~$1,150–$1,250 all-in.
- **Real negotiation exists:** recurring volume is acknowledged leverage — frequent corporate buyers are explicitly "in a strong position to negotiate fees" and delivery fees "can be negotiated on larger orders" ([Catering By Design](https://www.catering-by-design.com/blog/corporate-catering-value-guide/), [ezCater](https://www.ezcater.com/lunchrush/office/catering-order-minimums-service-fees/)).

## 3. Why it still fails as the *hackathon* vertical — three structural problems

**Problem 1 — The phone-necessity premise is broken. This is the fatal one.** **75% of catering orders already happen online; only ~25–30% come by phone** ([ezCater](https://www.ezcater.com/lunchrush/restaurant/introduction-to-catering-how-big-is-the-opportunity/)). ezCater has already digitized exactly this procurement flow — searchable menus, posted per-head prices, online ordering, reviews. The challenge thesis is "the real price is only available by phone, to whoever has the patience to ask" — that's true for a 6-person glass shop or a one-truck hauler; it is demonstrably *not* true for corporate lunch. A judge who has ever ordered office lunch knows ezCater exists. Your product demo would be competing with a mature marketplace on its home turf.

**Problem 2 — Quotes are not the same product.** A VIN defines an identical job; a photographed junk pile is at least the same pile. But three caterers quoting "lunch for 50" quote taco bars vs sandwich platters vs poke bowls — different food, different quantities per head, different quality tiers. Normalizing them invites exactly the brief's named weak-submission pattern: *"collect prices that aren't comparable and hide the differences in a polished dashboard."* You can force comparability by pinning an ultra-rigid spec ("identical boxed lunch, 1 sandwich + 1 side + 1 drink × 50"), but then the demo feels artificial and the interesting variation you're claiming to expose mostly disappears.

**Problem 3 — One call rarely produces a finished quote.** Catering sales conversations are menu consultations: dietary restrictions, menu selection, headcount confirmation, date availability, follow-up proposal by email. The natural cycle is call → emailed proposal → revision — poison for a live 3–5 minute demo that needs call → itemized number → negotiation → improved number.

**What catering has going for it** (to be fair): biggest market, best repeat-purchase economics, real fee opacity, real volume-based negotiation, natural document intake (old invoices, proposals), and a genuine B2B buyer (office managers) who would delegate this. Catering is a plausible *future config file* for the platform — a good company direction, aimed at the wrong challenge.

# Part 2 — The three-way verdict

Same weights as the glass-vs-junk scorecard (comparability 25, negotiation authenticity 20, demo story 15, doc intake 10, citable pain 10, phone-necessity 10, execution risk 5, differentiation 5):

| Criterion | Wt | Auto glass | Junk removal | Catering |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quote comparability | 25 | **10** — VIN = identical job | 7 — truck-fraction judgment | **3** — different food ≠ same product |
| Negotiation authenticity | 20 | **9** — price-matching is trade norm | 8 — informal haggling real | 6 — real but volume-based, slow-cycle |
| Demo story / relatability | 15 | 7 — needs ADAS explainer | **9** — instantly legible | 6 — relatable but low drama |
| Document intake | 10 | 8 — VIN + rival quote (doubles as leverage) | **10** — photos are the job | 8 — invoices/proposals |
| Citable pain numbers | 10 | **9** — $400→$850, 9-in-10 cars | 6 — fee ranges | 5 — fees exist but modest % |
| Phone-necessity | 10 | **8** — cash-pay quoting is phone-native | 6 — LoadUp online pricing | **2** — 75% already online (ezCater) |
| Execution risk (lower = better score) | 5 | 7 — half-day taxonomy prep | **10** — zero prep | 4 — menu loop hard to simulate cleanly |
| Differentiation vs other teams | 5 | **9** — likely unique | 6 — research-obvious pick | 7 — probably unique, for bad reasons |
| **Weighted total** | 100 | **88.6** | **77.0** | **48.6** |

**Final recommendation, definitive:**

1. **Build: auto glass (windshield for a known VIN).** Wins on the criteria the brief explicitly grades — airtight comparability, real-world negotiation behavior, citable hidden-fee numbers, defensible phone-necessity — and will be unique in the room.
2. **Fallback: junk removal** — if the team won't invest the half-day of glass domain prep or lacks a usable VIN. A confident junk demo beats a shaky glass demo.
3. **Retire catering as the hackathon vertical; keep it as the roadmap's platform slide** ("the same engine, config-swapped, later addresses the $109B recurring corporate catering market"). That sentence extracts all of catering's value at zero risk.

The rest of this document assumes **auto glass**. Every step is identical for junk removal except the config file and personas — that's the point of the architecture.

# Part 3 — Pathway to final product ("this, then that, then this")

Sequenced by dependency. Each milestone has: what you do → what must be true before moving on (gate). Timeline assumes a standard ~2-day hackathon plus prep time beforehand; shift proportionally if yours differs.

### Phase 0 — Before the hackathon (this weekend's prep, ~1 day total)

**Step 0.1 — Freeze the decision (1 hr).** Team answers the two decision-rule questions (half-day prep commitment? real VIN available?). Write the frozen scenario in one paragraph: vehicle, VIN, cash-pay, city, camera-equipped. *Gate: nobody reopens the vertical question after this.*

**Step 0.2 — Validation sprint (half day).** Manually call 5 local glass shops with the frozen VIN scenario. Log per call: answered? quoted in one call? itemized? mentioned calibration unprompted? all-in total? open to price-match? objection to an assistant calling? *Gate: ≥3 usable quotes collected. Output doubles as your demo's real-market benchmark numbers and persona dialogue material. If fewer than 3 shops quote by phone → trigger fallback to junk removal (same sprint takes 2 hours with photos).*

**Step 0.3 — Write the vertical config (2–3 hrs, from the validation data).** One YAML/JSON containing: job-spec taxonomy (VIN, glass type OEM/aftermarket, ADAS present, mobile vs in-shop, ZIP), fee taxonomy (glass, calibration, mobile fee, moldings/clips, disposal, tax), local benchmark range, red-flag rules (quote >30% below benchmark; calibration omitted on camera-equipped VIN), negotiation levers (verified rival total, schedule flexibility, in-shop vs mobile downgrade), forbidden moves (invented bids, fake urgency, booking anything). Also draft the junk-removal config now — it's your "config, not code" demo flourish. *Gate: every fee heard in validation calls maps to a schema line item.*

**Step 0.4 — Design the three counterparty personas on paper (2 hrs).** Each gets a private cost model: cost floor, target margin, fee schedule, concession ladder, refusal conditions, friction style. Suggested trio: (a) national-chain-style premium — polite, firm, price-matches verified written quotes within 10%; (b) independent lowballer — cheap headline, evasive about calibration until forced, interrupts; (c) rushed mobile operator — vague ranges, pushes callback, needs "manager approval" to concede. *Gate: for each persona you can answer "under exactly what condition does its price move?" Prices must never move without a trigger.*

### Phase 1 — Hackathon day 1 morning: text-only closed loop (the single most important milestone)

**Step 1.1 — Schemas first (1 hr).** JobSpec, Quote + QuoteLineItem (with provenance: which transcript second each number came from), CallOutcome (quote / callback / decline), VerifiedFact (the negotiation-leverage whitelist), Recommendation.

**Step 1.2 — Counterparty agents in text (2 hrs).** Three LLM personas from step 0.4, each with its private cost model in its system prompt, responding turn-by-turn — no scripted dialogue.

**Step 1.3 — Buyer agent in text (2 hrs).** Presents the frozen spec identically, forces itemization ("does that include calibration?"), confirms numbers, ends every conversation in a structured CallOutcome.

**Step 1.4 — Negotiation + ranking logic (2 hrs).** Deterministic core: leverage validator (only VerifiedFacts with transcript references can be spoken), one negotiation play (verified rival all-in total → price-match request), red-flag rules from config, scoring function (all-in price, completeness, binding status, red flags), report generator with citations. LLM only phrases; policy decides.

*Gate for Phase 1: one command runs spec → 3 text conversations → extraction → 1 successful negotiation with a traceable trigger → ranked cited report. **If this loop doesn't close by end of day-1 morning, cut scope until it does — everything after this is decoration on a working core. This gate is the difference between a submission and a fragment.***

### Phase 2 — Day 1 afternoon/evening: voice

**Step 2.1 — ElevenLabs Agents for the counterparties.** Port the three persona prompts; distinct voices *and* distinct friction behaviors (interruption, evasion, callback push).

**Step 2.2 — ElevenLabs Agent for the buyer/caller** with tool-calling: log_line_item, confirm_number, end_call_with_outcome — quotes get written to your backend *during* the call, which is your live-demo visual.

**Step 2.3 — Voice intake interview** (the Estimator): collects the spec fields conversationally, handles "I don't know" gracefully, produces the same JobSpec JSON.

**Step 2.4 — Conversation hardening:** AI-disclosure response ("Yes — I'm an AI assistant calling on behalf of a customer; I have the full vehicle details, can I get a quote?"), barge-in handling, number verification ("just to confirm — $585 all-in, including calibration?").

*Gate: one full voice call per persona, each ending in a structured outcome; the "are you a robot?" moment rehearsed and working.*

### Phase 3 — Day 2 morning: document intake + evidence surface

**Step 3.1 — Document path:** photo of VIN plate/insurance card (or a competitor's written quote — ideal, since it doubles as negotiation leverage) → vision model → same JobSpec JSON → user confirmation screen. *Gate: both intake paths emit byte-identical schema.*

**Step 3.2 — Evidence UI:** comparison table (all-in totals, line items, red flags), negotiation timeline (original → trigger → revised), click-a-number-hear-the-transcript-moment, final plain-language recommendation. This is where judges see trust, so spend real time here — it's the report requirement made visible.

*Gate: every number on screen traces to a transcript reference; zero hand-entered prices.*

### Phase 4 — Day 2 afternoon: demo hardening (do not skip for features)

**Step 4.1 — Golden-run rehearsals:** run the full loop 5+ times; fix the flakiest transition each time.
**Step 4.2 — Backup recordings** of every call segment, cued and ready — latency or a bad LLM turn during judging must not kill you.
**Step 4.3 — Demo script (3–5 min):** pain (real validation-call numbers) → voice + document intake → confirmed spec → play call highlights: the lowballer's hidden calibration fee exposed, the disclosure moment, the price-match negotiation with the on-screen trigger log → ranked evidence report → 30-second config-swap flourish (glass YAML vs junk YAML side by side).
**Step 4.4 — Judge Q&A prep:** why voice (validation data: X of 5 shops quote only by phone), why honest (leverage validator demo — try to make it lie, watch it refuse), why comparable (VIN), why real negotiation (persona cost models revealed after the calls).

*Gate: two consecutive flawless rehearsal runs, plus a backup-only run-through that also works.*

### The dependency chain in one line

**Freeze vertical → validate with 5 real calls → config + personas on paper → schemas → text loop closed ← everything depends on this → voice personas → voice buyer → intake interview → document path → evidence UI → rehearse → backups → script.**

### Definition of done (unchanged from the consolidation doc)

One uninterrupted run: photos/doc + voice interview → one confirmed spec → three live persona calls, distinct styles, structured endings → one negotiation where price measurably drops from a validated verified quote → ranked transcript-cited report → disclosure moment shown → config swap shown. All 14 compliance-checklist rows green. Nothing more.
