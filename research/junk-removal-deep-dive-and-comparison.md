## 1. The same three-layer clarification

| Layer | Junk-removal version |
|---|---|

## 2. Yes, this is also a real industry — the numbers

- **Market size:** US junk removal generated about **$10.7B in 2023**, projected to reach **$14.2B by 2028** (~5.9% CAGR) ([Gitnux industry statistics](https://gitnux.org/junk-removal-industry-statistics/)). Same order of magnitude as auto glass and roughly half the brief's $20B moving market.
- **Fragmentation — the shape the challenge wants:** **independent operators hold ~55% of the market**; the branded side is led by 1-800-GOT-JUNK (~12% national share), Junk King, and College Hunks Hauling Junk ([Gitnux](https://gitnux.org/junk-removal-industry-statistics/)). The long tail is thousands of one-truck, phone-first operators — "two guys and a truck" businesses that will never adopt quoting software but always answer the phone.
- **Unit economics of the incumbents:** 1-800-GOT-JUNK locations average **$2.5–3.4M revenue per franchise** ([Franzy franchise analysis](https://franzy.com/franchises/1800gotjunk), [Curbwaste](https://www.curbwaste.com/how-to-start-a-junk-removal-business)) — this is a high-margin trade where consumers routinely overpay, which is precisely the negotiation headroom your agent exploits.

**Who pays:** renters at move-out, homeowners decluttering, landlords between tenants, families handling estate cleanouts, small businesses clearing offices. It's a one-off purchase for consumers but a **recurring** one for property managers and realtors — that's the B2B expansion.

## 3. How pricing actually works — and where the opacity lives

**The pricing unit is the truck fraction**, which is exactly what makes photos such a natural intake document: 2026 rates run roughly **$100–$175 minimum (1/8 load), $150–$300 (quarter), $250–$550 (half), $550–$1,000 (full truck)**, with most jobs landing $150–$350 ([Housecall Pro pricing guide](https://www.housecallpro.com/resources/junk-removal-pricing/), [Angi](https://www.angi.com/articles/how-much-does-junk-removal-cost.htm)). Single items price flat: couch $75–$200, mattress $70–$160, fridge $100–$200 ([Housecall Pro](https://www.housecallpro.com/resources/junk-removal-pricing/)).

**The documented hidden-fee taxonomy** (your config file, essentially):

- **Stairs/access fees:** $25–$100 depending on floors and weight; apartments above the 3rd floor can add **$50–$100 per floor** ([Dropcurb](https://dropcurb.com/blog/how-much-does-junk-removal-cost)). Perfect for the demo scenario "3rd-floor walk-up, no elevator."
- **Item-type surcharges:** mattresses, refrigerants, e-waste, and tires carry their own transfer-station disposal fees, priced as extra line items ([Housecall Pro](https://www.housecallpro.com/resources/junk-removal-pricing/)).
- **Material density:** heavy debris (concrete, dirt) is disposal-fee dense — a half truck can out-cost a full truck of furniture.
- **Service level:** curbside pickup runs **20–30% below** full-service hauling ([Housecall Pro](https://www.housecallpro.com/resources/junk-removal-pricing/)) — which doubles as a truthful negotiation concession ("we'll bring it to the curb ourselves").
- **The bait pattern:** estimates typically exclude access difficulty, drive time, disposal type, and extra loads ([LoadUp](https://goloadup.com/blog/guides/how-much-junk-removal-cost/)) — the junk-removal version of "the price on the truck is not the price on the phone."

**The sight-unseen problem, junk-removal edition:** the market leader 1-800-GOT-JUNK gives an **exact price only on-site** — phone and web estimates are ranges ([1-800-GOT-JUNK pricing](https://www.1800gotjunk.com/us_en/how-our-pricing-works)). That's the industry's own admission that an unstructured phone description isn't quotable. Your agent's counter is the photographed, itemized, access-annotated spec — a phone quote *with* the information content of a site visit. That is a genuinely sharp product argument.

**Honest complication (know this before you pitch):** the same fact cuts the other way. If the biggest brand won't firm-quote by phone at all, your demo's "binding-ish phone quote" mostly comes from independents — true in reality, but a judge may ask. Also, online-first players like **LoadUp** already advertise upfront, no-onsite-visit pricing ([LoadUp](https://goloadup.com/blog/guides/how-much-junk-removal-cost/)) — a partial digitization threat that doesn't kill the thesis (55% of the market is independent phone operators) but weakens "the price only exists by phone" slightly, versus auto glass where cash-pay phone quoting is universal.

## 4. Business models, concretely

**Model A — Consumer success fee.** Same Rocket Money / BillShark precedent (35–60% of verified savings). Works identically: agent surfaces the $414 real total behind a "$299" quote, negotiates the stairs fee off with a verified rival bid, takes a cut of transcript-proven savings. Weaker than glass on ticket size (typical job $150–$350, so absolute savings are $50–$150 — the fee is small per transaction).

**Model B — Property-manager / landlord turnover service (the recurring business).** A property manager with 300 units does cleanouts constantly and today calls the same one or two haulers out of habit. An agent that re-competes every cleanout across the local independent market, with auditable quotes, is a genuine procurement product with repeat purchase. Adjacent expansions: estate-cleanout coordination (attorneys, realtors), retail/office cleanouts.

**Model C — Provider-side upsell (later):** the "structured brief" your agent produces (photos + access + item list) is more valuable to a hauler than a marketplace lead; provider-side subscription for qualified, spec-complete jobs is a plausible second revenue side — with the buyer-agency conflict managed carefully.

**Incumbents/analogs:** 1-800-GOT-JUNK / Junk King / College Hunks (branded supply, on-site pricing), LoadUp (online upfront pricing, no negotiation), Thumbtack/Angi (lead-gen forms that sell your number to 5 sales teams — the anti-pattern your product replaces), Rocket Money/BillShark (business-model analog).

## 5. Head-to-head: Junk Removal vs Auto Glass

Weighted for what the judges grade (comparability & negotiation authenticity heaviest), scored 1–10:

| Criterion | Wt | Junk removal | Auto glass | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quote comparability (the killer question) | 25 | 7 — photos + truck-fraction is good but still involves volume judgment a judge can poke | **10 — VIN defines the job exactly** | Glass's decisive edge |
| Real-world negotiation authenticity | 20 | 8 — haggling is normal among independents; curbside downgrade is a clean truthful lever | **9 — price-matching written quotes is standard trade practice** | Both strong |
| Demo relatability / story | 15 | **9 — everyone understands a pile of junk and a stairs fee; photos on screen are instantly legible** | 7 — needs 20 seconds of ADAS explanation | Junk's decisive edge |
| Document intake naturalness | 10 | **10 — photos ARE the job** | 8 — VIN photo + competitor quote (which doubles as leverage) | Junk edge; glass's doc is strategically richer |
| Citable pain numbers | 10 | 6 — $10.7B market, fee ranges, but no famous stat | **9 — $400→$850 calibration jump, 9-in-10 new cars, conflicted 33% incumbent** | Glass edge |
| Phone-necessity defensibility | 10 | 6 — LoadUp's online pricing and GOT-JUNK's on-site-only model both complicate it | **8 — cash-pay glass quoting is phone-native** | Glass edge |
| Execution risk / domain prep | 5 | **10 — zero prep, easy personas** | 7 — half a day of fee-taxonomy prep | Junk edge |
| Differentiation vs other teams | 5 | 6 — the "obvious smart pick" many research-driven teams will find | **9 — near-certain to be unique** | Glass edge |
| **Weighted total** | 100 | **77.0** | **88.6** | |

### The comparison in plain language

**Junk removal is the better *show*.** Photos of a couch and a mattress on screen, a "3rd-floor walk-up" fee appearing in real time, a $299 quote unmasked as $414 — any judge follows it with zero explanation. Intake is unbeatable (photos are literally the job), execution risk is minimal, and the personas are easy to write because everyone has met these businesses.

**Auto glass is the better *argument*.** Its comparability is airtight (same VIN = same job, by definition — no normalization to defend), its negotiation move (price-match a verified written quote) is something real shops do daily, its hidden fee has a famous citable number attached and a growth trend (9-in-10 new cars), its incumbent has a nameable conflict of interest (Safelite as both #1 retailer and claims administrator for 180+ insurers), and its business path (cash-pay success fee → fleet procurement) is cleaner. It will also almost certainly be the only auto-glass submission in the room.

**Where each one loses:** junk removal's weak points are exactly the two questions skeptical judges ask most — "are these quotes really comparable?" (truck-fraction is a judgment call) and "does this market really live on the phone?" (LoadUp prices online; the market leader only quotes on-site). Auto glass's weak points are softer story-stakes (no hostage furniture, no move-out deadline drama) and a half-day of domain prep the team must actually do.

### Verdict

**Auto glass by a clear margin on substance (88.6 vs 77.0); junk removal keeps the edge on pure theater and execution safety.**

Decision rule — answer two questions honestly:

1. *Will the team spend half a day internalizing the glass fee taxonomy and rehearsing shop-persona vocabulary?* If no → junk removal, and don't look back; a confidently executed junk demo beats an under-rehearsed glass demo.
2. *Do you have access to a real VIN (any teammate's car) for the validation calls and demo?* If no → junk removal.

If both answers are yes, **build auto glass**: it wins on the criteria the brief explicitly grades (comparable quotes, genuine negotiation, provable pain with real numbers, honest constraint design) and on differentiation. Either way, the engine, schemas, personas framework, evidence architecture, and demo structure from the consolidation doc are identical — this choice changes one config file and your narration, which is itself the strongest proof of the "config, not code" requirement. A closing flourish for the demo: show *both* config files side by side, whichever one you built.
